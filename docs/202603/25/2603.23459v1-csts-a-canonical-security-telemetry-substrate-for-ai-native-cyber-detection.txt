# CSTS: A Canonical Security Telemetry Substrate for AI-Native Cyber Detection 

Abdul Rahman arahman@alum.howard.edu 

Abstract — AI-driven cybersecurity systems often fail under cross-environment deployment due to fragmented, event-centric telemetry representations. We introduce the Canonical Security Telemetry Substrate (CSTS), an entity-relational abstraction that enforces identity persistence, typed relationships, and temporal state invariants. Across heterogeneous environments, CSTS improves cross-topology transfer for identity-centric detection and prevents collapse under schema perturbation. For zero-day detection, CSTS isolates semantic orientation instability as a modeling, not schema, phenomenon, clarifying layered portability requirements. 

Index Terms — Cybersecurity telemetry, Entity-centric mod-eling, Knowledge graphs, Security data integration, Repre-sentation learning, Zero-day threat detection, Anomaly detec-tion, Telemetry normalization, Cross-environment generaliza-tion, Enterprise security architecture 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Machine learning and graph-based methods are now widely used in cybersecurity for tasks such as lateral move-ment detection, anomaly discovery, and zero-day threat detection [1]–[4]. In controlled settings, these approaches can achieve strong performance; however, operational de-ployment remains difficult. Models that work well in one enterprise environment frequently degrade when moved to another, even when the underlying threat behaviors are similar. This degradation often persists across changes in topology, telemetry vendors, and logging configurations, suggesting that the limiting factor is not model architecture alone. A primary source of deployment friction is teleme-try representation. Enterprise security data is fragmented across heterogeneous vendor schemas, event-centric formats, and environment-specific pipelines. Normalization frame-works such as the Open Cybersecurity Schema Framework (OCSF) [5] reduce syntactic inconsistency and improve cross-vendor field alignment, but they remain fundamentally event-focused: higher-order constructs required for portable learning systems—persistent entity identity, typed relational semantics, and explicit temporal state—are typically re-constructed downstream on a per-environment basis. As a result, detection systems repeatedly re-implement identity stitching, relationship extraction, and feature logic for each deployment, creating brittle coupling between detection logic and telemetry idiosyncrasies. We view this as a representational gap : the absence of a canonical abstraction layer between raw telemetry ingestion and downstream analytics. Without such a substrate, porta-bility failures conflate multiple mechanisms. Some failures arise from schema instability (e.g., missing fields, renamed columns, vendor drift). Others arise even when schema is aligned: features derived from relational structure can change their meaning under domain shift, producing semantic ori-entation instability where class-conditional feature direction reverses across environments. Distinguishing these mech-anisms is essential for building robust AI-native security systems. In this work, we introduce the Canonical Security Teleme-try Substrate (CSTS) , an entity-first, relationship-explicit rep-resentation layer that formalizes (i) persistent canonical iden-tities, (ii) typed interactions as first-class relations, and (iii) temporally indexed state suitable for downstream statistical, graph, and learning-based detectors. CSTS is designed as a stable integration boundary: heterogeneous telemetry sources are mapped into canonical entities and typed relationships via thin adapters, and detection systems consume CSTS primitives rather than vendor-specific event fields. We empirically evaluate CSTS under controlled and ex-ternal settings. In a synthetic two-environment benchmark (Env A →Env B) designed to isolate representational effects, CSTS materially reduces transfer degradation for identity-centric detection (lateral movement) relative to an event-centric baseline and remains operable under targeted schema perturbations that cause the baseline pipeline to fail. For flow-centric zero-day detection, CSTS preserves schema alignment but reveals a distinct portability boundary: direc-tional stability of graph-derived statistics under domain shift remains a separate modeling challenge. Finally, on public real-log corpora we include external robustness/smoke tests and a producer-divergence case study (TC E3) to validate pipeline behavior under realistic telemetry heterogeneity and to surface cross-producer distributional mismatch under leakage-safe thresholding. This paper makes three contributions: 1) Canonical substrate. We formalize CSTS as a gov-erned entity-relational telemetry layer with explicit identity persistence, typed relationship semantics, and temporal indexing. 2) Portability decomposition. We articulate a portability framework that separates schema stability (represen-tation and ingestion invariants) from semantic ori-entation stability (feature direction invariance under domain shift). 3) Empirical evidence. We provide controlled transfer and schema-perturbation experiments demonstrating improved portability for identity-centric detection, and

Modern cybersecurity architectures aggregate telemetry from heterogeneous sources including network infrastructure, identity providers, endpoint agents, cloud control planes, and threat intelligence feeds. Although advances in normaliza-tion frameworks and machine learning–based detection have improved large-scale analysis, persistent deployment friction remains. Integration overhead, representational instability, and cross-environment degradation continue to limit reliable AI adoption in production environments. We review three relevant foundations: (i) event-centric nor-malization frameworks, (ii) machine learning–based detec-tion systems, and (iii) empirical deployment gaps observed in practice. 

A. Event-Centric Normalization 

Enterprise telemetry is fragmented across vendors, schemas, and collection pipelines. To address syntactic in-consistency, normalization frameworks such as the Open Cybersecurity Schema Framework (OCSF) [5], STIX/TAXII, Elastic Common Schema (ECS), and related standards align field names and event structures across products. These frameworks improve interoperability by standardizing event-level attributes such as timestamps, source/destination identifiers, event categories, and metadata fields. In practice, normalization reduces parsing complexity and enables cross-tool ingestion into SIEM and data lake environments. However, event-centric normalization primarily solves 

schema alignment . It does not provide higher-order semantic abstraction. Persistent entity identity, typed relationships among entities, temporal behavioral state, and longitudinal role modeling are typically reconstructed downstream within detection pipelines. Consequently, identity stitching is repeat-edly reimplemented for each deployment, graph-construction logic becomes bespoke and tightly coupled to specific de-tection models, and behavioral-aggregation pipelines vary across environments. This fragmentation increases opera-tional overhead and introduces instability into downstream learning systems. Thus, while normalization frameworks reduce syntactic heterogeneity, they do not define a canonical entity-relational substrate suitable for stable AI-native modeling across het-erogeneous deployments. 

B. Machine Learning–Based Detection 

Machine learning methods are widely applied to cyberse-curity tasks including anomaly detection, lateral movement identification, zero-day discovery, ransomware detection, and insider threat monitoring. Supervised classifiers, unsuper-vised anomaly detectors, graph neural networks, autoen-coders, and metric learning approaches have demonstrated strong performance in controlled settings. Graph-based models capture relational structure among users, hosts, processes, and network flows. Autoencoder architectures model baseline behavior and identify deviations indicative of novel attacks. Metric learning frameworks struc-ture latent representations to improve separation of attack classes. Despite their architectural sophistication, these systems often rely on environment-specific preprocessing, feature engineering, and identity-resolution rules. Relationship se-mantics and behavioral features are often tightly coupled to telemetry idiosyncrasies. As a result, feature definitions become unstable across deployments, transfer performance degrades under topology or vendor changes, and model portability often requires retraining or manual adaptation. These effects collectively undermine the reliability of AI-based detection systems in heterogeneous enterprise envi-ronments. These limitations indicate that improvements in model architecture alone are insufficient without a stable representational boundary between ingestion and detection. 

C. Deployment Gaps 

Operational deployment reveals recurring friction beyond model accuracy. Integrating new telemetry sources requires adapter development, identity reconciliation, and reconstruc-tion of higher-order behavioral abstractions. Vendor updates and schema drift introduce ongoing maintenance burden. Furthermore, graph structures used by detection systems are often rebuilt independently within each pipeline rather than derived from a shared canonical representation. This increases operational complexity and creates tight coupling between ingestion logic and modeling logic. Cross-environment generalization remains particularly challenging. Models trained in one enterprise topology may degrade when deployed in another due to differences in identity semantics, role distributions, and telemetry cover-age—even when schema fields are nominally aligned. This suggests that portability failures may arise from deeper semantic instability rather than schema mismatch alone. Collectively, these gaps indicate the absence of a canonical telemetry substrate that formalizes persistent identity, typed relationships, and temporal state independent of vendor-specific event formats. III. P ROBLEM STATEMENT 

Existing AI-driven cybersecurity systems lack a canonical, entity-centric telemetry representation that enables stable deployment and cross-environment generalization. Event-centric normalization aligns fields but does not formalize:  

> •

Persistent canonical identity across heterogeneous sources,  

> •

Typed relationship semantics suitable for graph and relational modeling,  

> •

Temporally indexed behavioral state evolution,  

> •

Compatibility with embedding- and representation-based learning paradigms. As a consequence, detection pipelines repeatedly reconstruct behavioral abstractions, graph construction remains ad hoc and model-specific, schema drift and telemetry variation erode portability, and integration overhead slows enterprise adoption. Together, these factors create structural friction that limits scalable and generalizable AI deployment. We define the central problem as follows: 

How can enterprise security telemetry be repre-sented in a canonical, entity-relational form that enables stable deployment, cross-topology gener-alization, and compatibility with advanced learn-ing paradigms without requiring per-environment reconstruction of behavioral features? 

IV. R ELATED WORK 

AI-driven cybersecurity systems operate at the intersection of enterprise telemetry architecture, schema normalization, graph-based reasoning, and learning-based detection. Sub-stantial advances have been made across these domains. However, most prior work implicitly assumes the availability of stable, semantically coherent telemetry representations. We synthesize existing research and identify a persistent rep-resentational gap: the absence of a vendor-agnostic, entity-first, relationship-explicit substrate designed explicitly to support portable AI deployment across heterogeneous enter-prise environments. 

A. Telemetry Security Architecture and Enterprise Deploy-ment Context 

Enterprise telemetry infrastructures are increasingly recog-nized as high-value assets requiring layered security controls. Kalibjian emphasizes that telemetry post-processing envi-ronments contain sensitive and strategically valuable data, arguing for encryption, key management, and compliance-aligned protections for telemetry at rest and in motion [6]. Zegeye and Odejobi propose a cybersecurity architecture for telemetry networks modeled after ICS/SCADA systems, emphasizing segmentation, zone-based control, and bound-ary management across enterprise and field layers [7]. Dean 

et al. further extend this perspective with a networked telemetry security architecture integrating layered controls across distributed enterprise domains, positioning telemetry infrastructure as a foundation for advanced analytics and AI-driven monitoring [8]. These works provide critical operational realism by clari-fying how telemetry is generated, transmitted, and secured. However, telemetry is treated primarily as protected data-in-motion rather than as a formally defined representational layer for AI systems. Canonical identity persistence, typed inter-entity semantics, and substrate-level temporal abstrac-tion are not explicitly addressed. 

B. Telemetry Normalization and Observability Frameworks 

Normalization frameworks such as the Open Cybersecurity Schema Framework (OCSF) [5], Elastic Common Schema (ECS) [9], and OpenTelemetry [10] have significantly im-proved cross-vendor interoperability through standardized event schemas and attribute alignment. These frameworks reduce ingestion friction, simplify SIEM integration, and enable consistent field naming across heterogeneous sources. Rongali demonstrates how OpenTelemetry instrumentation can be extended with enriched spans and security-oriented attributes to enhance detection fidelity [11]. Such efforts show that structured instrumentation improves contextual signal quality. However, normalization frameworks remain fundamen-tally event-centric. They standardize event syntax but do not formalize persistent entity identity across sessions and vendors, canonical inter-entity relationships, or longitudi-nal behavioral state. Higher-order abstractions required for AI-native detection are therefore reconstructed downstream within environment-specific pipelines. Normalization reduces syntactic variance but does not ensure semantic stability. 

C. Telemetry Representation and Scalable Security Analytics 

Representation-level innovation has sought to elevate telemetry beyond raw event streams. Taylor et al. introduce SysFlow, a compact telemetry format that lifts low-level sys-tem events into a flow-centric, object-relational abstraction linking processes, files, and network endpoints [12]. SysFlow improves interpretability and reduces storage overhead by compressing event granularity into relational summaries. While aligned with the goal of relational abstraction, SysFlow focuses primarily on host-level telemetry and does not generalize across heterogeneous enterprise domains such as identity providers, SaaS platforms, cloud control planes, or threat intelligence systems. Nor does it define governance mechanisms for cross-domain identity persistence or canoni-cal relationship ontologies suitable for stable AI deployment. Thus, representation compression improves efficiency but does not establish a universal substrate for cross-environment portability. 

D. Learning-Based Detection and Graph-Oriented Security Systems 

Machine learning is central to modern intrusion detection. Okonkwo surveys advanced intrusion detection approaches incorporating behavioral modeling and AI-enhanced anomaly detection in enterprise telemetry networks [13]. Zero-day detection research emphasizes anomaly modeling and be-havioral generalization for detecting previously unseen at-tack patterns [1], [2]. Metric learning approaches structure latent representations to improve discrimination of malicious behavior [2], [14], [15]. Graph-based intrusion detection systems and knowledge graph security models encode entities and interactions to enable contextual reasoning, attack path reconstruction, and campaign analysis [16]–[19]. These systems demonstrate that relational structure is essential for detecting multi-stage adversarial activity such as lateral movement. However, most graph and learning-based systems derive entity graphs and behavioral features from event streams in environment-specific ways. Identity resolution, relationship extraction, and feature engineering remain tightly coupled to vendor schemas and telemetry peculiarities. As a result, portability across enterprises is limited, and deployment frequently requires retraining or re-engineering. The limiting factor increasingly lies in representational instability rather than model capacity. 

E. Telemetry Collection and Dataset Generation 

Holeman et al. introduce SETC, a containerized frame-work for repeatable exploit execution and telemetry collec-tion to support controlled dataset generation [20]. SETC ad-vances reproducibility and systematic evaluation of defensive models under known attack scenarios. However, while dataset generation improves experimen-tal rigor, it does not resolve representational heterogene-ity in real enterprise environments. Telemetry collected in controlled settings still requires identity reconciliation and relational abstraction when deployed into heterogeneous op-erational ecosystems. 

F. Feature Drift, Domain Adaptation, and Deployment Fragility 

Feature and concept drift are well-documented challenges in operational intrusion detection, where evolving traffic patterns and attack behaviors degrade model performance over time [21]. Broader surveys of distribution shift em-phasize that deployed machine learning systems require continuous monitoring and adaptation in non-stationary en-vironments [22]. In enterprise cybersecurity, distribution shift is com-pounded by schema variation, vendor heterogeneity, teleme-try incompleteness, and identity inconsistencies across de-ployments. Existing mitigation strategies focus on retraining, adaptive ensembles, or adversarial robustness, but rarely address representational stability at the telemetry substrate level. This recurring deployment fragility suggests that the prin-cipal bottleneck in AI-driven cybersecurity is architectural rather than purely algorithmic. 

G. Synthesis: The Representational Gap 

Across telemetry security architecture, normalization frameworks, scalable representation formats, graph-based de-tection systems, and ML deployment research, three themes consistently emerge: 1) Enterprise telemetry is operationally complex and strategically sensitive, requiring secure and governed infrastructure [6]–[8]. 2) Advanced detection methods rely on stable relational and behavioral abstractions, including persistent entity identity and cross-domain contextual modeling [2], [14], [15]. 3) Event-centric normalization frameworks improve in-gestion but do not formalize a canonical entity-relational substrate with governed identity persistence and temporal continuity [5], [11], [12]. No surveyed work defines a cross-domain, entity-first, relationship-explicit telemetry layer with persistent iden-tity resolution, typed relational semantics, temporal state continuity, and representational stability contracts designed explicitly for AI portability. This representational gap motivates the Canonical Security Telemetry Substrate (CSTS), introduced next. V. D ESIGN PRINCIPLES OF CSTS The Canonical Security Telemetry Substrate (CSTS) is designed as an AI-native representation layer that formalizes how heterogeneous enterprise security telemetry is structured before downstream modeling. Unlike event-centric normal-ization frameworks (e.g., OCSF [5], ECS [9], OpenTeleme-try [10]), CSTS is not limited to field alignment. Instead, it establishes stable abstractions for persistent identity, typed relationships, temporal continuity, and governed evolution so that detection models consume a consistent entity-relational state rather than brittle vendor-specific event fields. CSTS is guided by five design principles. 

A. Principle 1: Persistent Entity Identity 

CSTS adopts an entity-first paradigm: security-relevant objects are first-class constructs rather than implicit artifacts reconstructed from events. Canonical entity types include users, hosts, processes, files, network endpoints/flows, cre-dentials, cloud resources, binaries, and externally sourced indicators. Each entity is assigned a persistent canonical identifier intended to remain stable across telemetry sources and time. This principle targets a central deployment failure mode in event-centric systems: identity instability. In heterogeneous environments, a single logical entity can appear under mul-tiple identifiers across vendors and over time (e.g., hostname aliases, principal-name variants, IP reassignment). When identity is not stabilized, downstream pipelines repeatedly re-stitch entities, producing inconsistent graph construction and degraded cross-environment transfer. CSTS makes identity resolution a substrate responsibility so detection logic can remain vendor-agnostic and topology-portable. 

B. Principle 2: Explicit Relationship Semantics 

Security-relevant behavior is fundamentally relational. CSTS therefore materializes typed relationships as ex-plicit substrate constructs rather than deriving them ad hoc inside model pipelines. Relationship types are semanti-cally constrained (e.g., AUTHENTICATES TO , EXECUTES ,

SPAWNS , CONNECTS TO , WRITES , PRIV ESCALATES )and define permissible source and target entity classes. By enforcing relationship semantics at the substrate layer, CSTS yields a canonical entity-relational graph that is stable across deployments. Downstream models consume pre-materialized interaction structure rather than rebuilding topology from raw logs, improving reproducibility and re-ducing environment-specific feature re-engineering. This also enables graph-based learning and reasoning methods to oper-ate over consistent semantics without bespoke reconstruction logic. C. Principle 3: Temporal Continuity and Behavioral State 

Enterprise security telemetry is inherently temporal: many detection paradigms depend on deviations from histori-cal baselines, sequences of actions, and evolving roles. Event normalization records timestamped events but typi-cally leaves longitudinal state and temporal joins to down-stream pipelines. CSTS incorporates temporal continuity at the substrate boundary by representing entity and relationship state as time-indexed updates. This supports rolling aggregation win-dows, behavioral baselining, and trajectory-based reasoning in a uniform way across environments. By centralizing temporal semantics, CSTS reduces duplication of temporal aggregation logic across detection systems and improves cross-topology comparability of behavioral features. 

D. Principle 4: Feature Stability and Schema Governance 

A recurring cause of enterprise ML fragility is feature instability induced by schema drift, vendor updates, and environment-specific telemetry variance. When features de-pend directly on raw fields, modest upstream changes can invalidate models or require costly re-integration. CSTS addresses this through schema governance and feature stability contracts. Canonical behavioral categories (e.g., frequency, diversity, rarity/novelty, privilege spread, role deviation) are defined over canonical entities and rela-tionships and versioned independently of raw event formats. Changes in upstream telemetry are absorbed by adapter-layer mappings and canonicalization policies rather than by modifying model interfaces. This governance approach supports backward compatibility, reduces retraining pressure, and improves operational portability. 

E. Principle 5: Deployment Decoupling and Extensibility 

CSTS decouples telemetry integration from detection logic. Vendor feeds are mapped through thin adapters into canonical entities and relationships; downstream consumers then operate on the uniform substrate. This reduces on-boarding friction, confines environment-specific work to the mapping layer, and enables incremental adoption. CSTS is intentionally model-agnostic while remaining ML-compatible: the substrate supports supervised learn-ing, unsupervised anomaly detection, graph learning, metric learning, and embedding-based retrieval. Stabilizing the rep-resentational boundary also enables future extensions such as agentic investigation and response workflows over shared canonical state, without requiring structural redesign. 

F. Summary of Design Principles 

In combination, these principles shift the representational burden from individual detection pipelines to a shared substrate. Persistent identity, typed relationships, temporal continuity, governed evolution, and deployment decoupling establish CSTS as a foundation for portable, enterprise-ready security analytics across heterogeneous environments. D. Learning Objects and Representation Views over CSTS 

While CSTS is defined as a time-indexed attributed multigraph over canonical entities and typed relationships, downstream learning systems typically do not consume the full substrate directly. Instead, they operate on bounded relational-temporal units derived from the substrate. To make this interface explicit, we define a learning object in CSTS as a semantically governed, time-bounded view over canonical entities, relationships, and associated state used as the input unit for downstream statistical, graph, or representation-learning models. This extension is consistent with CSTS’s existing role as an entity-first, relationship-explicit substrate designed to support anomaly detection, metric learning, graph-native models, and embedding-compatible AI systems [1], [2], [15]. Formally, let 

Gt = ( Vt, E t)

denote the CSTS graph at time t, with canonical entities and typed relationships as defined above. A CSTS learning object is a tuple 

o = ( Vo, E o, T o, X o, P o, ϕ o),

where:  

> •

Vo ⊆ Vt is a bounded set of canonical entities;  

> •

Eo ⊆ Et is a bounded set of typed relationships among those entities;  

> •

To = [ ta, t b] is a bounded temporal support interval;  

> •

Xo is the set of canonical attributes and derived behav-ioral summaries over To; 

> •

Po records provenance, adapter lineage, and confidence metadata inherited from the substrate;  

> •

ϕo is an optional focalization map specifying the central entity, interaction, or motif around which the object is constructed. The purpose of this construction is not to introduce a new primitive distinct from canonical entities and relationships, but to provide a stable model-consumption contract over existing CSTS primitives. In other words, CSTS remains ontologically entity-relational, while learning objects provide a bounded computational interface for downstream models. Several classes of learning objects arise naturally in CSTS. a) 1) Entity-state objects.: An entity-state object rep-resents the temporally indexed behavioral state of a single canonical entity over a bounded interval. For example, a host-state object may summarize a host’s authentication neighborhood, process activity, file access diversity, and network role over the previous 24 hours. A user-state object may summarize login cadence, peer-host diversity, privilege spread, and role deviation relative to historical baselines. Entity-state objects are especially natural for identity-centric tasks such as lateral movement detection, insider threat modeling, and behavioral risk scoring. 

b) 2) Interaction-state objects.: An interaction-state object represents a focal typed interaction together with its source-target context. For example, a focal CONNECTS TO 

edge between a process or host and an external destina-tion may be enriched with the source entity’s prior state, the destination neighborhood, recent authentication activity, associated credentials, and bounded graph statistics over an interval To. This object class is particularly useful for flow-centric anomaly detection, zero-day threat detection, and edge-level scoring tasks, especially in settings where graph-aware context improves cross-topology transfer [1]. 

c) 3) Subgraph-state objects.: A subgraph-state object 

represents a bounded typed neighborhood around a focal en-tity or interaction, typically at one-hop or two-hop depth, to-gether with explicit temporal support. Such objects preserve local relational structure without requiring the full enterprise graph to be processed at once. They are appropriate for graph neural networks, neighborhood aggregation methods, motif-sensitive detectors, and hybrid tabular-graph encoders. In CSTS terms, they expose local relational geometry under the substrate’s typing and provenance constraints, aligning with graph-aware zero-day detection and more recent graph-contrastive intrusion detection formulations [1], [23]. 

d) 4) Motif-state objects.: A motif-state object repre-sents a recurrent local behavioral pattern expressed over canonical entities and typed relationships. Examples in-clude fan-out authentication bursts, process-to-file write cascades, privilege-boundary traversals, repeated remote-execution chains, and short-window bipartite expansion pat-terns consistent with ransomware-like behavior. The purpose of the motif-state class is to elevate repeated relational structure to a first-class learning unit , allowing models to reason over local attack grammar rather than only isolated events or aggregate counts. These object classes preserve CSTS’s entity-first seman-tics while making explicit the units by which downstream models consume canonical telemetry. A detector operating over CSTS need not reconstruct object boundaries from raw vendor events; instead, object construction is governed by substrate-level canonicalization, temporal semantics, and relationship typing. 

e) Representation views.: To support modern represen-tation learning, we further define a view of a CSTS learning object as a semantics-preserving transformation 

v : o 7 → eo

such that eo preserves the canonical identity, typed relational interpretation, and temporal validity of the underlying object while varying its presentation to a downstream model. Views do not alter the ontology of CSTS; rather, they expose mul-tiple valid presentations of the same underlying relational-temporal state. Examples of valid CSTS views include:  

> •

Attribute-masked views: selected non-critical at-tributes in Xo are hidden or perturbed while preserving canonical entity identity and relation typing;  

> •

Neighborhood-subsampled views: a bounded subset of a local neighborhood is sampled while preserving the focal entity or interaction and core typed relations;  

> •

Adjacent-window views: temporally nearby slices of the same object are formed to capture behavioral con-tinuity or mild temporal variation;  

> •

Partial-source views: one telemetry producer or modal-ity is omitted while preserving canonical identity and recoverable relational meaning;  

> •

Modality-restricted views: tabular-only, temporal-only, graph-only, or hybrid projections derived from the same underlying object. Not every transformation is a valid view. A valid CSTS view must satisfy: 1) Identity preservation: canonical entity identity for focal entities must remain unchanged; 2) Relational validity: typed relationships must remain semantically valid under domain/codomain constraints; 3) Temporal consistency: the transformed object must preserve a coherent temporal support interval or a semantically admissible sub-interval; 4) Provenance traceability: the transformation must pre-serve lineage, source metadata, and confidence anno-tations sufficient for auditability; 5) Semantic admissibility: the transformation may re-duce observability or alter resolution, but it must not convert the object into one with a different underlying behavioral interpretation. These constraints are important because they connect representation learning to the portability decomposition de-veloped later in the paper. CSTS is intended to guaran-tee schema stability and representational invariance at the substrate layer. Learning-object and view semantics extend that guarantee upward into model construction by specifying what bounded units may be compared, embedded, recon-structed, or contrasted across deployments. In this sense, CSTS does not merely normalize telemetry; it defines a governed representational basis for substrate-native anomaly detection, novelty detection, metric learning, contrastive learning, and graph-temporal reasoning over persistent se-curity state [2], [15], [24], [25]. This formalism is particularly useful in the zero-day setting. A flow-centric detector may treat a focal network flow together with its surrounding host, process, credential, and neighborhood context as an interaction-state object, while a novelty or contrastive learner may compare multiple valid views of that same object across time, partial-source visibility, or deployment environment. Likewise, identity-centric detectors may operate over entity-state objects whose temporal neighborhoods are canonically comparable across enterprises. In each case, CSTS reduces dependence on brittle vendor-specific event fields and instead exposes stable learning units grounded in canonical entities, typed rela-tionships, temporal continuity, and provenance-preserving abstraction. Accordingly, learning objects and representation views should be understood as a Tier-4 extension of CSTS rather than a separate modeling layer. They make explicit how embedding-compatible systems consume the substrate, clar-ify the semantics of contrastive and metric objectives over canonical security telemetry, and prepare the ground for substrate-native zero-day detection architectures built di-rectly on CSTS [1], [2], [15], [23], [24]. 

E. Schema Governance and Evolution 

Long-term portability requires explicit schema gover-nance. Each substrate release is versioned: 

Schema v (11) Adapters declare compatibility with specific versions. 

Backward Compatibility Rules: 

1) Additive attribute extensions are permitted. 2) Renaming requires alias mapping. 3) Removal of required fields mandates deprecation peri-ods. Schema evolution must preserve entity identity invariants, relationship semantics, and temporal consistency. 

F. Representational Guarantees 

CSTS enforces representational invariants at the telemetry layer: 1) Persistent canonical identity across ingestion bound-aries, 2) Environment-independent relationship typing, 3) Explicit temporal continuity, 4) Governance-backed schema evolution. These guarantees stabilize the representational bound-ary between telemetry ingestion and downstream modeling. CSTS does not guarantee downstream model generalization; rather, it isolates portability constraints to the representation layer, allowing modeling-level properties to be analyzed independently. 

Implications 

This section formalized CSTS as a time-indexed, entity-centric multigraph with explicit identity, relationship, tempo-ral, and governance invariants. By elevating security teleme-try from raw event streams to stable canonical entities and relationships, CSTS provides a structurally consistent substrate for downstream analytics. The formal definitions ensure that cross-source integration, temporal reasoning, and provenance preservation are enforced at the architectural level rather than deferred to model-specific preprocessing. Crucially, CSTS shifts the stability requirement from machine learning pipelines to the telemetry substrate itself. Rather than compensating for drift, schema fragmentation, and identity inconsistency through retraining or adaptive heuristics, CSTS defines structural guarantees that persist across heterogeneous deployments. In doing so, it reframes cybersecurity AI fragility as a representational problem with a systematic architectural solution. VII. C ASE STUDIES 

Security analytics systems frequently fail in deployment not because detection logic is weak, but because detec-tion pipelines are tightly coupled to environment-specific telemetry schemas and brittle feature engineering layers. Even well-established techniques for lateral movement detec-tion, anomaly detection, and ransomware identification often require substantial re-engineering across enterprises. CSTS addresses this limitation by separating detection logic from telemetry representation, providing a stable entity-relational substrate on which diverse analytical methods can operate consistently. To illustrate the implications of this architectural sep-aration, we reinterpret several established detection prob-lems through the lens of CSTS. The objective is not to propose new detection algorithms, but to demonstrate that once telemetry is expressed as interactions over persistent canonical entities and typed relationships, seemingly distinct security tasks reduce to identifying structured deviations in an evolving graph. This reframing clarifies portability bound-aries and reduces per-environment feature reconstruction. 

A. Lateral Movement Under CSTS 

Lateral movement detection traditionally relies on authen-tication sequences, credential reuse patterns, remote ses-sion behavior, and multi-host traversal analysis. Prior work demonstrates strong performance using user behavioral fea-tures engineered from authentication telemetry [4]. However, these pipelines typically reconstruct entity graphs implicitly and rely on environment-specific aggregation logic. Within CSTS, lateral movement is interpreted as role deviation in a constrained entity-relational graph. Canon-ical entities such as User , Host , Credential , and 

Process are connected through typed relationships in-cluding AUTHENTICATES TO , EXECUTES , and SPAWNS .Lateral movement corresponds to the emergence of traversal paths inconsistent with the historical role topology of a user or credential entity. Signals described in [4]—such as unusual host adjacency, abnormal session patterns, or deviations from typical login rhythms—map naturally to graph deviations in CSTS: novel 

User →Host adjacencies, rapid ego-network expansion, or traversal chains exceeding typical path lengths. Rather than modeling isolated authentication events, CSTS treats lateral movement as subgraph expansion within a persistent identity graph. Because identity and relationship semantics are stabi-lized at the substrate layer, the detection problem becomes one of structural deviation rather than vendor-specific field engineering, improving portability across environments. 

B. Zero-Day Threat Detection Under CSTS 

Zero-day detection research frequently frames the task as anomaly detection over network telemetry augmented with structural context [1], or as a separation between anomaly detection and novelty detection via metric-learning autoen-coders [2]. These approaches rely on capturing deviations from learned behavioral baselines while maintaining robust-ness to previously unseen attack classes. Under CSTS, zero-day detection can be viewed as identifying relational deformation within the geometry of canonical entities and interactions. In graph-and-flow ap-proaches [1], flows are combined with topology-aware fea-tures such as centrality and neighborhood structure. In CSTS, these structural properties arise directly from the canonical graph, where persistent identity and typed relationships de-fine a stable interaction topology. Operationally, anomaly detection corresponds to identi-fying edge or interaction patterns that cannot be recon-structed under learned substrate-consistent role geometry, aligning with autoencoder-based formulations [26]. Novelty detection can be expressed as metric separation between established behavioral manifolds and emergent relational configurations [2]. The portability benefit is that learning shifts from raw identifiers (e.g., vendor-specific fields or IP strings) to structural properties of canonical entities. Detection thus focuses on deviations in interaction structure rather than surface-level telemetry artifacts. 

C. Ransomware as Motif Emergence 

Ransomware detection is often based on file entropy signals, process bursts, or signature-like heuristics. Within CSTS, ransomware is reinterpreted as the emergence of a characteristic relational motif within the entity graph. A canonical ransomware pattern typically includes ex-ecution of an anomalous binary, followed by rapid and high-density WRITES /MODIFIES relationships between a process entity and many file entities within short temporal windows, often accompanied by privilege boundary crossing. In graph-theoretic terms, this manifests as abrupt degree expansion and repeated bipartite interaction between a single process node and a large file neighborhood. Cross-temporal ransomware research emphasizes detecting campaigns under changing conditions [3]. CSTS supports this objective by stabilizing identity, temporal state, and re-lationship semantics across deployments. Motif-based detec-tion can therefore focus on structural replication patterns [27] rather than vendor-specific file system fields, improving cross-environment consistency. Across these case studies, CSTS provides a unifying abstraction: lateral movement, zero-day activity, and ran-somware can all be interpreted as statistically improbable relational configurations within a persistent entity graph. By stabilizing identity, relationship semantics, and temporal state at the substrate layer, CSTS reduces integration friction and clarifies the boundary between representational stability and model-level generalization. Detection logic operates over canonical interaction structure rather than brittle telemetry schemas, improving reproducibility and portability across heterogeneous enterprise environments. VIII. D EPLOYMENT MODEL AND TIERED ADOPTION 

CSTS is designed for incremental adoption rather than wholesale infrastructure replacement. Enterprise security en-vironments vary significantly in maturity, tooling, and inte-gration capability. A viable representational substrate must therefore support staged deployment, allowing organizations to progressively increase semantic richness without disrupt-ing existing pipelines. We define a five-tier adoption model that enables enter-prises to move from basic event normalization toward fully embedding-compatible, AI-native telemetry abstraction. 

A. Tier 0: Event Normalization 

Tier 0 represents alignment with existing telemetry nor-malization frameworks such as OCSF [5], Elastic Common Schema (ECS) [9], and OpenTelemetry [10]. At this stage, organizations standardize field names, event categories, and log formats across vendors. Normalization improves interoperability and reduces pars-ing overhead but remains event-centric. Entities are not persistently resolved, relationships are implicit, and temporal state is reconstructed downstream. Tier 0 therefore reduces syntactic inconsistency but does not address representational stability required for portable AI systems. CSTS treats Tier 0 as compatible but insufficient. 

B. Tier 1: Entity Resolution 

Tier 1 introduces persistent canonical identity across telemetry sources. Raw observations are mapped to canonical entity types (e.g., User , Host , Process ) with globally stable identifiers. This layer implements the canonical reso-lution function defined in the formal substrate specification, ensuring that semantically equivalent observations across heterogeneous systems map to a consistent identity. Entity resolution is the minimal viable CSTS substrate. Once identity persistence is enforced, downstream analytics can operate over stable actor representations rather than ephemeral event records. This addresses a primary source of deployment fragility identified in ML-based security systems [21], [22], where schema variation and inconsistent identi-fiers degrade model portability. 

C. Tier 2: Relationship Materialization 

Tier 2 materializes typed relationships between canonical entities, forming an explicit entity-relational graph. Authen-tication, process execution, file modification, network flow, and cloud interaction events are transformed into directed edges with defined domain and codomain constraints. This tier aligns directly with graph-based detection paradigms demonstrated in prior work on lateral movement and zero-day detection [1], [4]. Instead of reconstructing relationships within each analytic pipeline, CSTS defines them once at the substrate level. Materializing relationships reduces repeated feature engi-neering and enables structural pattern detection, including motif analysis consistent with established graph-theoretic foundations [27]. At Tier 2, the telemetry substrate becomes analytically expressive rather than merely normalized. 

D. Tier 3: Temporal Modeling 

Tier 3 incorporates explicit temporal state modeling into the substrate. Entities and relationships are represented as time-indexed structures with validity intervals, rolling ag-gregation windows, and state transitions. This enables be-havioral baselining, sessionization, and cross-entity temporal joins at the substrate layer rather than within model-specific code. Temporal modeling is critical for anomaly detection and novelty detection systems [2]. Without explicit temporal semantics, models must infer behavioral continuity from fragmented logs. CSTS instead enforces temporal explic-itness, enabling rolling behavioral state representation that remains consistent across deployments. Temporal modeling strengthens cross-environment com-parability by ensuring that behavioral state and aggregation semantics are consistent across deployments. While this does not guarantee downstream model generalization, it reduces representation-induced variability associated with evolving telemetry distributions and schema drift [21], [22]. 

E. Tier 4: Embedding-Compatible Extensions 

Tier 4 extends CSTS to support embedding-compatible representations for AI-native modeling. At this stage, canon-ical entities and relationships expose structured feature con-tracts suitable for graph embedding, contrastive learning, metric learning, and autoencoder-based anomaly detection. Embedding-compatible extensions do not replace earlier tiers; they build upon stable identity, typed relationships, and temporal state. Prior work demonstrates the effectiveness of metric learning and representation learning for zero-day detection [2], [14], [15]. However, such models often require bespoke feature engineering tied to specific telemetry schemas. By defining embedding interfaces at the substrate level, CSTS enables model portability across heterogeneous enter-prise environments. The representational boundary ensures that learned embeddings reflect canonical relational structure rather than vendor-specific artifacts. Tier 4 extends CSTS from structural normalization into an AI-ready substrate. By defining embedding-compatible interfaces over canonical entities and relationships, CSTS enables learning systems to operate on stable relational structure rather than vendor-specific artifacts. This preserves portability while supporting anomaly detection, novelty de-tection, motif reasoning, and representation learning within a unified architectural boundary. IX. P ORTABILITY DECOMPOSITION FRAMEWORK 

CSTS is designed to stabilize telemetry representation across heterogeneous enterprise environments. However, rep-resentational stability alone does not guarantee model porta-bility. To reason precisely about deployment behavior, we decompose portability into three distinct layers: schema sta-bility, representational invariance, and semantic orientation stability. This decomposition enables us to distinguish failures caused by ingestion or schema variation from failures caused by modeling assumptions under domain shift. 

A. Schema Stability a) Definition (Schema Stability).: A detection pipeline satisfies schema stability if feature definitions and aggrega-tion logic remain unchanged under field renaming, deletion, format variation, or vendor-specific schema changes. Schema instability manifests when upstream telemetry modifications break feature extraction, graph construction, or preprocessing pipelines. Event-centric normalization frame-works (e.g., OCSF [5]) reduce syntactic drift but do not eliminate representational fragility. CSTS enforces schema stability by requiring that raw telemetry variations be absorbed at the adapter layer, preserv-ing canonical entities and relationships as the stable interface to downstream models. 

B. Representational Invariance a) Definition (Representational Invariance).: A teleme-try representation satisfies representational invariance if: 1) Entity identity persistence is maintained across inges-tion boundaries, 2) Relationship type semantics are environment-independent, 3) Temporal ordering and state continuity are preserved. Representational invariance ensures that the structural meaning of entities and relationships does not depend on vendor-specific encodings or topology-specific artifacts. CSTS enforces representational invariance through canon-ical identity resolution, typed relationship constraints, tem-poral modeling, and governance-backed schema evolution. Importantly, representational invariance guarantees struc-tural comparability of entity graphs across environments but does not guarantee that learned features behave identically under domain shift. 

C. Semantic Orientation Stability 

Even when schema stability and representational invari-ance hold, learned features may invert or degrade under distribution shift. 

a) Definition (Semantic Orientation Stability).: A fea-ture f exhibits semantic orientation stability between en-vironments EA and EB if the sign of its class-conditional expectation remains invariant:

If the sign reverses, a feature that was positively corre-lated with malicious behavior in one environment becomes negatively correlated in another. This phenomenon represents a modeling-level portability failure rather than a representa-tional one. 

D. Portability Failure Modes 

The decomposition above clarifies three distinct failure regimes: 1) Schema Failure: Feature extraction breaks due to field drift. 2) Representational Failure: Entity identity or relation-ship semantics are inconsistent across environments. 3) Orientation Failure: Learned feature direction re-verses under domain shift despite stable representation. CSTS is designed to eliminate the first two categories by enforcing structural invariants at the telemetry layer. However, orientation stability remains a property of the learning task and data distribution. 

E. Implications for Detection Tasks 

This framework explains why different detection paradigms exhibit different portability behavior. Identity-centric tasks such as lateral movement detection rely heavily on stable relational topology. When canonical identity and relationship semantics are preserved, feature orientation tends to remain stable across environments. In contrast, anomaly- and geometry-based tasks such as zero-day detection depend on global distributional prop-erties of interaction structure. Even with representational invariance, latent geometric assumptions may shift across enterprise topologies, producing orientation instability. This distinction motivates our experimental evaluation: CSTS should eliminate schema and representational failures, while residual degradation reflects modeling-level semantic instability rather than telemetry fragmentation. X. E XPERIMENTAL VALIDATION

We empirically examine three stability properties derived from the formal definitions in Section VI: representa-tional invariance, schema stability, and semantic orientation stability. H1 (Representational Invariance). Enforcing canonical entity persistence and typed relational semantics improves cross-topology generalization for identity-centric detection tasks. 

H2 (Schema Stability). Confining raw-schema variability to thin canonical adapter layers prevents detection collapse under field-level perturbation. 

H3 (Semantic Orientation Boundary). Representational invariance does not guarantee semantic orientation stability under domain shift. G. Semantic Orientation Instability Under Domain Shift 

The contrasting behavior between lateral movement (LM) and zero-day threat detection (ZDT) under cross-topology transfer reveals an important structural distinction. LM de-tection is primarily an identity-centric deviation task: it detects abnormal role transitions and entity interaction pat-terns. ZDT, in contrast, is a distributional anomaly detection problem that relies on statistical deviations in flow- and connectivity-derived features. Under CSTS, schema alignment is preserved across envi-ronments. Canonical entities, typed relationships, and tem-poral aggregation windows remain stable. Feature extraction is performed over identical entity-relational abstractions in both Env A and Env B. This eliminates schema-level fragility and ensures that feature definitions do not depend on vendor-specific field names, log formats, or identifier conventions. However, the ZDT experiments demonstrate that schema stability does not imply semantic stability. Several graph-derived features (e.g., connectivity counts, two-hop expan-sion measures, and degree-based statistics) exhibit cross-domain polarity inversion: the direction of their class-conditional expectation reverses between environments. In other words, features that are positively associated with anomalous behavior in the training environment become neg-atively associated in the target environment, despite identical canonical representation. This phenomenon isolates a precise portability bound-ary. CSTS guarantees representational invariance—persistent identity, typed relational semantics, and temporally indexed state—but does not guarantee semantic orientation stability under distributional shift. The inversion observed in ZDT therefore reflects a modeling-level instability rather than a failure of canonical schema alignment. in other words, CSTS isolates semantic orientation instability as a modeling phenomenon rather than a schema artifact. This layered interpretation clarifies that portability in cy-bersecurity AI decomposes into at least two separable com-ponents: (i) schema stability, which CSTS directly enforces, and (ii) semantic feature stability under domain shift, which remains an open modeling challenge. By stabilizing the rep-resentational boundary, CSTS enables more precise diagnosis of cross-environment degradation mechanisms, separating ingestion-level fragility from distributional modeling effects. 

H. Summary 

Taken together, these experiments demonstrate that porta-bility in cybersecurity AI decomposes into layered stability properties. Identity persistence and typed relational semantics (H1) materially improve cross-topology transfer for identity-centric tasks. Schema stability (H2) prevents collapse under telemetry perturbation. However, semantic orientation stabil-ity (H3) remains a distinct modeling challenge that is not resolved by canonical representation alone. These results validate the central thesis of this work: deployment fragility is fundamentally representational, yet portability itself is multi-layered. By formalizing and em-pirically isolating these layers, CSTS provides a principled foundation for analyzing and improving cross-environment cybersecurity detection systems. XI. D ISCUSSION 

This work argues that deployment fragility in AI-driven cybersecurity systems is fundamentally representational. Modern detection pipelines frequently entangle schema as-sumptions, identity resolution, relational context construc-tion, and modeling logic within a single workflow. As a result, performance degradation under cross-topology de-ployment or telemetry evolution is often attributed to model-ing weakness when the underlying cause is representational instability. CSTS introduces a formally defined representational boundary that separates telemetry ingestion from detection logic. By enforcing entity persistence, typed relationship semantics, and temporal state continuity, CSTS targets repre-sentational invariance and schema stability. The experimental results clarify both the strengths and the limits of this architectural shift. 

Layered Portability: Representation vs. Semantics 

The empirical findings support a layered theory of porta-bility:  

> •

Identity-centric transfer benefits from representa-tional invariance. In lateral movement detection, per-sistent canonical identities and typed relationships re-duce cross-topology degradation, supporting H1: sta-bilizing identity and interaction semantics improves transfer across heterogeneous environments. • Schema perturbation exposes ingestion fragility in event-centric pipelines. Renaming or removing baseline-critical raw fields causes catastrophic collapse when features are coupled directly to vendor schemas. CSTS confines such variability to thin adapters, preserv-ing detection functionality and supporting H2.  

> •

ZDT reveals a distinct portability boundary at the semantic layer. Although CSTS enforces representa-tional invariance and schema stability, several graph-derived statistics exhibit polarity inversion under do-main shift. This violates semantic orientation stability despite identical canonical representation across envi-ronments, supporting H3 and isolating the failure mode as modeling-level semantic variation rather than schema misalignment. Taken together, the results indicate that portability decom-poses into separable stability properties:  

> •

Schema and representational stability (enforced by canonical entity-relational abstraction and adapter iso-lation), and  

> •

Semantic orientation stability (dependent on feature construction, score calibration, and distributional align-ment under domain shift). CSTS directly addresses the first layer and isolates the second. By stabilizing representation, it converts ambiguous cross-environment failures into diagnosable semantic phe-nomena. 

Decoupling Representation from Detection 

A central architectural implication is the formal decou-pling of telemetry representation from detection logic:  

> •

Reduced coupling to raw schemas. Many deployed systems implicitly encode assumptions about field names, log formats, or vendor-specific semantics. CSTS externalizes these assumptions into adapters and a gov-erned substrate boundary.  

> •

Model-agnostic substrate compatibility. CSTS supports heterogeneous detection strategies—statistical anomaly detection, graph motif analysis [27], reconstruction-based methods [26], metric learning [2], and contextual graph modeling [1]—without direct dependence on raw telemetry schemas.  

> •

Representation as an independent robustness lever. 

In our experiments, baseline and CSTS pipelines use identical windowing and classifier configurations; ob-served differences arise from representational properties rather than algorithmic changes. This isolates substrate design as a standalone architectural mechanism for improving portability. 

Integration Complexity as a Systems Problem 

Enterprise detection systems often incur repeated engi-neering effort for schema alignment, identity stitching, and relational reconstruction. While normalization frameworks such as OCSF [5], ECS [9], and OpenTelemetry [10] stan-dardize event fields, they remain event-centric and do not formalize persistent entity identity or typed relationship semantics. CSTS reframes integration as a systems problem with explicit substrate governance:  

> •

From field alignment to entity-relational state. CSTS extends normalization into a governed, identity-stable, temporally indexed multigraph.  

> •

Centralized stability contracts. Once canonical state is materialized, detection logic operates on stable struc-tures rather than raw logs, shifting complexity from repeated analytic reconstruction to a centrally governed substrate with versioning and invariants.  

> •

Operational impact under evolution. Perturbation results illustrate the practical benefit: schema evolution impacts adapters rather than collapsing downstream detection behavior. 

Implications for AI-Native Security Architectures 

The results suggest that future AI-native security architec-tures should treat canonical entity-relational state as a first-class operational layer:  

> •

Persistent substrate as shared analytic context. 

Rather than reconstructing relational context inside each analytic workflow, systems can maintain a durable sub-strate over which anomaly detection, campaign correla-tion, and representation learning operate.  

> •

Semantic stability remains a modeling requirement. 

The ZDT findings underscore that canonical represen-tation alone does not guarantee semantic invariance under distribution shift. Robust systems must combine representational stability with orientation analysis, cal-ibration strategies, or domain-adaptive modeling.  

> •

CSTS makes remaining challenges explicit. CSTS does not eliminate the need for semantic alignment methods; it provides a stable boundary that makes their necessity observable and their evaluation more principled. In summary, CSTS reframes cybersecurity AI fragility as a layered systems problem: schema variability, identity frag-mentation, and semantic instability are distinct phenomena that require distinct solutions. By formalizing and empiri-cally isolating representational invariants, this work shows that stabilizing the telemetry substrate materially improves cross-topology robustness while clarifying the remaining modeling challenges. This layered perspective provides a principled foundation for portable, reproducible, and struc-turally coherent cybersecurity detection architectures. XII. F UTURE WORK

Substrate-Native Zero-Day Detection (ZDT v3)

Unlike sequence-based models that operate on flattened event streams, substrate-native temporal graph models can reason over structural evolution—capturing role transitions, motif emergence [27], and cross-entity propagation patterns. This may unify identity-centric detection [4], ransomware motif analysis [3], and relational anomaly detection [2] within a single modeling framework. Query and update canonical entity graphs directly,

Simulate hypothetical attack propagation scenarios,

Execute containment or mitigation actions with provenance-preserving updates. Collectively, these directions position CSTS not as a static schema proposal but as a foundational representational layer for AI-native cybersecurity architectures. By formalizing telemetry as identity-stable, temporally explicit relational state, CSTS enables portable detection systems, graph-native learning models, and agent-driven response frameworks that extend beyond event-centric pipelines. XIII. C ONCLUSION 

Modern cybersecurity systems increasingly rely on ma-chine learning, graph analytics, and automated reasoning to detect lateral movement, zero-day threats, and ransomware campaigns. Yet despite substantial algorithmic progress, de-ployment fragility persists. Detection pipelines remain tightly coupled to vendor-specific schemas, environment-dependent identifiers, and bespoke feature reconstruction logic. Cross-topology generalization degrades, integration overhead accu-mulates, and representational reconstruction dominates engi-neering effort. This work argues that the dominant constraint is represen-tational rather than algorithmic. Enterprise telemetry remains fragmented and event-centric, limiting its suitability as a stable substrate for portable AI-native reasoning. Field-level normalization frameworks improve interoperability, but they do not formalize persistent entity identity, typed relational semantics, or explicit temporal state evolution—properties necessary for structurally coherent detection architectures. To address this gap, we introduced the Canonical Security Telemetry Substrate (CSTS), an entity-first, relationship-explicit, temporally indexed representation layer that models enterprise security state as a governed multigraph with iden-tity stability and schema evolution controls. CSTS establishes an integration boundary between heterogeneous telemetry sources and downstream analytics, elevating canonical en-tities and typed interactions to first-class constructs. Empirically, we demonstrated three layered findings. First, for identity-centric detection tasks such as lateral movement, CSTS materially improves cross-topology transfer relative to event-centric baselines. Second, under targeted schema perturbation that causes raw event pipelines to collapse, CSTS preserves predictive signal by confining variability to thin canonical adapters. Third, in flow-centric zero-day detection, CSTS isolates a distinct portability boundary: while canonical schema alignment is preserved, semantic orientation stability of graph-derived features under domain shift remains a modeling-level challenge. This distinction clarifies that schema stability and semantic invariance are separable requirements for portable AI systems. These results motivate a reframing of AI-driven cybersecu-rity architecture. Detection robustness is not solely a function of model complexity, but of representational stability. Canon-ical entity-relational abstraction reduces ingestion fragility and improves cross-environment portability for identity-centric reasoning. At the same time, durable deployment across heterogeneous environments requires semantically sta-ble feature construction and calibration-aware modeling atop the substrate. CSTS does not replace existing normalization standards; it extends them into a higher-order representational layer com-patible with graph-native learning, embedding-based model-ing, and agent-driven response systems operating over shared canonical state. By formalizing this substrate and empirically evaluating its behavior under transfer and perturbation, this work articulates both the promise and the boundary of canonical telemetry representation. As cybersecurity systems continue to evolve toward AI-native architectures, stable, governed telemetry sub-strates will become foundational infrastructure. CSTS pro-vides a principled approach to constructing that infras-tructure—shifting detection from environment-specific fea-ture engineering toward structured reasoning over persistent, identity-stable, temporally explicit relational state. D. Views and Semantics-Preserving Transformations 

Contrastive and metric learning require multiple valid presentations of the same underlying object. We therefore define a view family over an object o as a collection 

V(o) = {eo1, . . . , eom}

> •

Attribute masking Mattr : hide or perturb selected non-core attributes;  

> •

Neighborhood subsampling Snbr : sample a bounded subset of neighbors while retaining focal identity and essential relation types;  

> •

Temporal offsetting Stime : construct nearby support intervals around the same focal object;  

> •

Source omission Ssrc : suppress one telemetry source or producer while preserving canonicalized structure;  

> •

Modality projection Πtab , Πtemp , Πgraph : expose only one modality of the same object;  

> •

Confidence-threshold pruning Pconf : remove low-confidence edges or attributes while preserving semantic consistency. 

2) View validity constraints: A transformation v ∈ V is valid only if: 1) focal identity is preserved; 2) typed relationship semantics are not violated; 3) temporal support remains coherent; 4) provenance remains recoverable; 5) the transformed object retains the same underlying behavioral interpretation. These constraints are important. Without them, contrastive learning may collapse into learning invariances that are oper-ationally meaningless or that erase the very threat signal one seeks to preserve. The value of contrastive learning in this setting is precisely that it can encourage semantically aligned views to occupy nearby regions of embedding space while preserving separation from semantically distinct behavior [15], [24], [25]. 

E. Positive, Negative, and Hard-Negative Policies 

To support contrastive and metric learning over CSTS, it is useful to define pairing policies over learning objects. 

1) Positive pairs: A pair (oi, o j ) is a positive pair when the two objects are distinct valid views of the same un-derlying behavioral unit or when they instantiate the same semantic role pattern under allowable deployment variation. Common examples include:  

> •

two views of the same interaction-state object under different masking patterns;  

> •

adjacent-window views of the same entity-state object;  

> •

subgraph-state objects representing the same canonical actor under different source visibility;  

> •

cross-environment objects occupying the same semantic role under stable CSTS typing. 

2) Negative pairs: A pair (oi, o j ) is a negative pair when the two objects represent distinct behavioral units whose relational or temporal semantics should remain separable in embedding space. Examples include:  

> •

benign host-state versus attack-associated host-state;  

> •

unrelated interaction-state objects with different role semantics;  

> •

dissimilar motif-state objects. 3) Hard negatives: Hard negatives are especially impor-tant in enterprise telemetry. These are object pairs with su-perficially similar aggregate statistics but materially different relational meaning. Examples include:  

> •

two flows with similar volume and duration but different process or credential context;  

> •

two hosts with similar degree statistics but different role neighborhoods;  

> •

two motifs with similar density but different typed edge composition. CSTS is particularly well-suited for hard-negative construc-tion because it makes entity identity, typed relations, and provenance explicit, allowing the pairing policy to use se-mantic context rather than only scalar similarity. This is also where metric-learning formulations remain useful: they help organize latent space geometry, especially for low-support or fine-grained attack classes [2], [14].

G. Open-Set and Zero-Day Implications 

The zero-day setting motivates a particularly important use of CSTS learning objects. Let o(f low ) denote an interaction-state object centered on a focal network flow. Under CSTS, this object can be expanded to include:  

> •

source and destination host state,  

> •

associated process and credential context,  

> •

one-hop or two-hop typed neighborhood,  

> •

rolling behavioral summaries over multiple windows,  

> •

provenance-aware missingness or visibility masks. A reconstruction-based anomaly detector may score such objects by their deviation from learned benign or expected geometry, consistent with prior autoencoder-based anomaly detection approaches [1], [26]. A metric or contrastive learner may further organize the embedding space so that known malicious behaviors form structured regions while seman-tically novel behavior lies at greater distance from both benign and known-malicious manifolds [2], [24]. This gives CSTS a direct role in zero-day and open-set architectures: the substrate defines the object semantics and view validity, while the downstream objective shapes the geometry. 

H. Why This Appendix Matters for CSTS 

This appendix clarifies that CSTS is not only a normal-ization layer but also a governed representation substrate 

for AI-native security learning. Canonical entities, typed relationships, temporal continuity, and provenance are the substrate primitives. Learning objects are bounded construc-tions over those primitives. Views are semantics-preserving transformations of those objects. Pairing policies and con-trastive objectives then operate over that governed space. This layered interpretation is important for portability. Schema stability and representational invariance are enforced at the substrate layer. Contrastive and metric objectives then operate on a learning space whose semantics are already stabilized. In this sense, CSTS does not solve all downstream generalization problems by itself, but it sharply reduces the representational ambiguity under which such models are otherwise trained and deployed. It also makes explicit how CSTS can support follow-on work in zero-day detection, graph-aware novelty detection, and contrastive/open-set cy-ber representation learning [1], [2], [15], [23], [24].
