# Collaborative AI Agents and Critics for Fault Detection and Cause Analysis in Network Telemetry 

Syed Eqbal Alam 1,2 and Zhan Shu 11Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, University of Alberta, Edmonton, Alberta, Canada 

> 2

SheQAI Research, Edmonton, Alberta, Canada 

Abstract— We develop algorithms for collaborative control of AI agents and critics in a multi-actor, multi-critic federated multi-agent system. Each AI agent and critic has access to classical machine learning or generative AI foundation models. The AI agents and critics collaborate with a central server to complete multimodal tasks such as fault detection, severity, and cause analysis in a network telemetry system, text-to-image generation, video generation, healthcare diagnos-tics from medical images and patient records, etcetera. The AI agents complete their tasks and send them to AI critics for evaluation. The critics then send feedback to agents to improve their responses. Collaboratively, they minimize the overall cost to the system with no inter-agent or inter-critic communication. AI agents and critics keep their cost functions or derivatives of cost functions private. Using multi-time scale stochastic approximation techniques, we provide conver-gence guarantees on the time-average active states of AI agents and critics. The communication overhead is a little on the system, of the order of O(m), for m modalities and is independent of the number of AI agents and critics. Finally, we present an example of fault detection, severity, and cause analysis in network telemetry and thorough evaluation to check the algorithm’s efficacy. 

Index Terms— AI agents, distributed optimization, optimal control, multimodal foundation models, generative AI, federated multi-agent system, large language models. 

## 1 Introduction 

Foundation models are generic generative artificial intelligence models trained on a vast corpus of data. They need significant computing infrastructure to train them. Moreover, they can be adapted to perform a wide variety of tasks [7]. Foundation models, such as large language and vision models, have shown remarkable capabilities to generate human-like text, code, videos, audio, language understanding, image classification, etcetera. Some of the examples of foundation models are OpenAI’s GPT-4, Google Deepmind’s Flamingo [4], Meta’s Llama3.2, Mistral, IBM’s Granite [14], to name a few. The foundation model’s generated and processed data type is called a modality. Based on the modalities, we categorize foundation models into unimodal and multimodal. Unimodal 1

foundation models generate and process one type of data or modality, such as generating text, whereas multimodal models generate and process several modalities, such as text, im-age, video, etc. Developing uni-modal foundation models is a challenging task that involves resolving data and security issues, fairness, model alignment, etc. Furthermore, sometimes, the model gives fictional outputs with high confidence (referred to as hallucination ). Devel-oping multimodal foundation models that support multiple modalities increases complexity several times more than uni-modal models because of processing different modalities’ data. Furthermore, it needs to address other issues of each modality it supports and their inte-gration issues. As a basic example, in the multimodal model for text, vision, and audio, for pre-processing input data, the text uses words as tokens; vision uses pixels as tokens; audio uses sound frequencies as tokens; creating these tokens involves different techniques. Instead of developing foundation models that work for many modalities, we can work with special-ized models that do specific tasks very well and then integrate these foundation models of desired modalities; this can be done through multi-agent collaboration. Each agent can have access to one or more foundation models; we call such agents AI agents . Recently, systems utilizing foundation model-based single autonomous agents are progressing towards multiple collaborative agents. Every agent of the foundation models-based multi-agent system can have a unique capability, and they collaboratively can provide emerging capabilities for mul-timodality. Moreover, using multiple collaborative agents increases the accuracy of the tasks’ outcome [12]. Most of the LLM-based multi-agent systems work is centered around text generation, and a few are in multimodal settings [9]. We propose stochastic algorithms to control the number of AI agents and critics in a federated multi-agent system. In the proposed model, multiple agents and critics collaborate with a central server to complete multimodal tasks and aim to minimize the overall cost to the system. The AI agents and critics have access to foundation models of different modalities or classical machine learning models. The central server keeps track of the number of active agents and critics at a time step and broadcasts a feedback signal 

for a modality in the system at each time step. Users pass their questions or queries to AI agents; an agent generates its response and sends it to an active critic. The critic then eval-uates the agent’s response and sends feedback to the agent. The agent considers the critic’s feedback and improves its response if it agrees with the critic’s evaluation. With feedback, critics can also include a score, a numeric value. For example, after receiving, the agent’s response, the critic evaluates it, assigns a score, and sends feedback with score to the agent. The agent considers the critic’s score and feedback and improves its response if the score is below a predefined threshold. If the score of the generated response is above the threshold, agents send it as final response for the task. The block diagram for essential components of AI agents and critics and their interactions is illustrated in Figure 1. Generative AI-enabled agents and critics have models (LLMs or LVMs), prompts to perform the task, and conver-sation history in their memory. In addition, the critics can also have evaluation criteria with different score weightage for response accuracy, task relevance, completeness, clarity, etc. Agents and critics make their decisions in a probabilistic way whether to participate at a time step or not. The response probabilities of an agent depend on its time-average active states, the (partial) derivatives of its cost function, and the feedback signals from the central server. Analogously, for the critics. The cost functions of an agent are coupled through its time-average active states for all modalities. We present an example of fault detection, severity, and cause analysis in network telemetry system. Network telemetry is an automated process to remotely collect and process network information [16]. It can monitor data flow across the network to identify any unusual traffic, anomaly, or fault. Furthermore, it improves networks performance, efficiency, reliability, and security [18]. Network telemetry is a building block for many network management applications [6], such as fault detection and localization [5], congestion control [13], network health monitoring [10], etc., more applications can be found at [11, 16]. Fault detection, severity, and cause analysis is one of the most important problems in networks for safe and reliable operation of the networks. We evaluate our model with an open-source network telemetry dataset available at [15]. We chose dataset 4 that contains tabular data in a comma-separated values (CSV) format and log files for port state changes. The data has three types of port state changes: Optical transceiver pull and reinsert, admin state change (down and up), and link state changes (plug-in and plug-out of fiber optics cable). The producer devices are named “leaf” and “spine” with numeric values, such as leaf1, spine2, etcetera. The CSV file has 99 columns, which include numeric and non-numeric data; to name a few— name, time, bandwidth, bytes-received, bytes-sent, CRC-errors, input-data-rate, input-drops, reliability, state, system-ram-memory, total-cpu-fifteen-minute , etcetera. Figure 2 illustrates the density plots of a few columns of all rows of the CSV file of dataset 4 of [15]. For better illustration, Figure 3 presents plots of a few columns for some of the data points. We evaluate the developed model for fault detection, severity, and cause analysis for the network telemetry dataset in two steps: (i) Fault detection and (ii ) Severity and Cause anal-ysis. In the fault detection step, we use two approaches: (a) agents enabled with classical machine learning techniques and (b) agents and critics enabled with Generative AI’s foun-dation models. In the Severity and Cause analysis step, we use agents and critics enabled with Generative AI’s foundation models. In classical machine learning, the Extreme Gradient Boosting (XG Boosting) model is used; it provides higher accuracy, F1 score, precision, and recall with faster processing than Generative AI’s foundation models in the fault detection phase. In classical machine learning based approach, agents are enabled with the Extreme Gradient Boosting model; however, critics are not used (it does not affect the model for-mulation). Nevertheless, we use both agents and critics in the Generative AI’s foundation model-based approaches for fault detection. In the fault detection step, we can use agents en-abled with foundation models, and then critics enabled with the XG Boosting or vice versa, but at the cost of higher execution time. One of the advantages of enabling agents with classical machine learning is the speed and accuracy of fault detection, which is crucial in segregating the faults faster. For fault severity and cause analysis, we use agents and critics enabled by foundation models. The developed model is suitable for real-time fault detection, severity, and cause analysis. ## 2 Notations and Problem description 

Let A = {A1, A 2, . . . , A N } be a set of N AI agents in a multi-agent system. Let every agent have access to foundation models, perception module (data input), and have a memory to store their responses/conversation history. These agents collaborate to complete tasks of several modalities, such as audio, text, video, sensor data, etc. Let the total number of modalities be m. Furthermore, let us assume that a central server tracks the number of active agents completing the tasks. We call such a setting as federated multi-agent system .The central server knows the desired number of agents required to complete the tasks of each modality; we call it the capacity of the modality . Let N1, N2, . . . , Nm denote the capacities of modalities 1, 2, . . . , m , respectively. Furthermore, we consider a few critics for each modality. The critics of the modality evaluate the quality of agents’ responses and provide feedback (with or without a score) to each response. Let the number of critics required to evaluate a task of modality j be denoted by Cj , for j = 1 , 2, . . . , m . Analogous to agents, each critic has access to a foundation model, perception module (data input), and has a memory to store their responses, queries, and agents’ responses. The critics can also follow an evaluation criteria for agent’s responses for accuracy, relevance to task, completeness and clarity. Let all critics are not required to be active at a time step, and let M1, M2, . . . , Mm denote the critics capacities of modalities 1, 2, . . . , m , respectively. ## 3 Algorithm Design 

This section presents the developed algorithms for AI agents, AI critics, and the central server. AI agents and critics are Generative AI’s foundation model or classical machine learning models enabled. AI agents have access to models of different modalities and complete multimodal tasks. Moreover, we consider that there are m sets of critics, and critics of each set evaluate the agents’ responses for one particular modality. Each AI agent runs its algorithm to calculate its probabilistic intent to participate in completing its (multimodal) tasks. Analogously, the critics run their algorithms to calculate their probabilistic intent to evaluate the responses of the AI agents and provide feedback on each response. After receiving feedback from the critics, agents update their responses if they agree with the evaluation. Notice that the algorithm can also be updated to include score with feedback. If the score is below a threshold, then the agent updates its response; otherwise, it sends the response as final. Moreover, the threshold could be set by the algorithm designer based on the prediction accuracy of the models. The central server keeps track of the number of active AI agents and critics at each time step. The feedback signal of j’th modality depends on its value at the previous time step, a step size, modality capacity, and the total number of active participants (agents or critics). The central server updates and broadcasts feedback signals for each modality to agents and critics at every time step. 3.2 Algorithm of AI critics 

We now present the algorithm of the critics. Analogous to the AI agents, the AI critics are also generative AI’s foundation model enabled. They receive responses from AI agents at each time step and calculate their probabilistic intent to be active or not to evaluate the agents’ responses at the next time step. AI critics evaluate AI agents’ responses and send feedback to them. With a minor update in the algorithm, the critics can also send scores with their feedback. ## 5 Experimental results: Fault detection and cause analysis in a multimodal network telemetry system 

We evaluate our model with an open-source network telemetry dataset available at [15] for fault detection, severity, and cause analysis. We chose dataset 4 that contains tabular data in a comma-separated values (CSV) format and log files for port state changes. The data has three types of port state changes: Optical transceiver pull and reinsert, admin state change (down and up), and link state changes (plug-in and plug-out of fiber optics cable). For fault detection, developed model is evaluated by two approaches: AI agents enabled with classical machine learning techniques (e.g., XG Boosting), and AI agents and critics enabled with large language models. We observe that agents enabled with XG Boosting provide faster results and higher accuracy, F1-score, precision, and recall than AI agents and critics enabled with LLMs. As described earlier, the CSV file of network telemetry dataset 4 of [15] has 99 columns, which include numeric and non-numeric data. Data are pre-processed before being accessible to AI agents and critics. The pre-processing step is presented in the following subsection. 

5.1 Pre-processing of network telemetry data 

The time column in the CSV file is in Unix nanosecond format, whereas the dates in log and event files are in Pacific Daylight time (PDT). To unify the formats, Unix nanosecond time is converted into Pacific Daylight time (PDT). Furthermore, all the numeric columns with zero variance are removed because they do not affect fault analysis. Using the time duration 21 of fault events presented in the event file of the dataset 4 of [15], we extract the fault data and add a fault column in the CSV file. An equal number of no-fault entries is added to the CSV file; the entries are labeled as “1” for fault and “0” for no fault, we call the dataset a 

balanced dataset . Furthermore, these entries are randomized. 

Fault detector agents enabled with the XG Boosting: For the XG Boosting, the balanced dataset with fault column (CSV file) is split into two parts—one is used for training and another for testing purposes. XG Boosting model is trained with 2/3 of the records, and the remaining records (test files) are split into smaller files. The XG Boosting trained model is saved and accessed by fault detector agents. At each iteration, active agents access a CSV test file to predict the faults. The resulting fault-classified data is saved as a CSV file. 

Fault detector agents and critics enabled with large language models (LLMs): 

AI agents and critics can access different large language models (LLMs), such as Llama3.2, DeepSeek-R1, Mistral, etc., for response generation and evaluation. For LLM-enabled agents and critics, the CSV data are converted into text queries and saved as CSV files for efficient processing by the LLMs. For the fault detection step with LLM-enabled agents and critics, the text queries CSV file is then converted into smaller chunks to pass to agents and critics for fault detection. At each iteration, an active agent processes a file and passes its responses to critics for feedback. An active critic then evaluates the agent’s responses, checks their correctness, and sends feedback to the agent. The agent then considers the critic’s feedback to improve its response; however, if the agent disagrees with the critic’s evaluation, it does not change the answer. ## 6 Conclusion 

We developed stochastic iterative algorithms to control the number of active AI agents and critics in a multi-agent system to complete multimodal tasks. The AI agents and critics are either classical machine learning or generative AI’s foundation model enabled. The AI agent’s cost functions are coupled through the active states of different modalities. The critic evaluates the response of the AI agents and sends feedback on the evaluation to the agent to improve its performance. The critics’ cost function depends on the task modality it evaluates. AI agents and critics collaborate with a central server that keeps track of the number of active AI agents and critics to perform a modality task. Additionally, the central server sends feedback signals to the network, which helps AI agents and critics calculate their probabilistic intent to be active in performing a modality task at a time step or not. Eventually, AI agents and critics obtain optimal values over long-term average active states. We used the developed algorithms for fault detection, severity, and cause analysis on a real-world network telemetry dataset. For fault detection, the agents enabled with the clas-sical machine learning approach have better accuracy, F1 score, precision, and recall than the large language model-enabled agents. Agents and critics were enabled by large language models for fault severity and cause analysis. The algorithms were evaluated with multiple large language models—Llama3.2, DeepSeek-R1, Microsoft’s Phi4, IBM’s granite3.2:8b, Mis-tral, and Llava:7b. It would be exciting to implement the algorithms on real network devices and detect the faults in real-time and analyze the causes. Moreover, the algorithm can be implemented in several application domains, such as smart energy systems, fault detection, 33 and cause analysis in industrial applications.
